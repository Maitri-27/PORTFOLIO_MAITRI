Personal Stories

Overcoming a Challenge — The Automation Wall

When I first encountered automation testing, I hit a wall hard. The syntax confused me, the frameworks felt overwhelming, and every tutorial assumed I already knew things I didn't. I could've stayed comfortable in manual testing. Instead, I gave myself 30 minutes every morning before work — just learning, breaking things, learning again. Three months later, I was writing stable scripts and teaching a junior tester the same ropes. The wall didn't move. I climbed it.

Learning a New Skill — Tools Don't Scare Me Anymore

I used to think being a QA meant knowing one set of tools really well. Then a project demanded Selenium, another needed Postman, another wanted Cypress. Each time, I had to learn fast and apply faster. Now I don't see a new tool as a threat — I see it as a new language to pick up. The skill underneath the tools — systematic thinking and curiosity — never changes. That's what I actually learned.

Team Moments — The Developer Who Hated My Bug Reports (Then Didn't)

There was a developer on my team who'd push back on almost every bug I raised. Tense at first. But I started changing how I reported — adding screen recordings, steps in plain language, impact context. Slowly, our dynamic flipped. He started pinging me before shipping features, asking "anything look off to you?" Quality isn't one person's job. That collaboration taught me it's a culture you build, one bug report at a time.

Career Transition — I Didn't Plan to Be a QA Engineer

I didn't grow up dreaming of test cases. My path here was a series of "let me try this" moments. But when I found QA, something clicked — it combined my attention to detail, love for problem-solving, and genuinely caring about the end user's experience. I wasn't just testing software. I was making sure someone's workday, checkout, or medical record didn't break. That purpose? That's what kept me in it.

Work Stories

The Bug That Almost Shipped on a Friday

It was 4:47 PM on a Friday. The team was wrapping up. I ran one final regression check — a habit I'd built even when everyone said "it's fine." And there it was: a critical payment flow bug that would have hit thousands of users over the weekend. Nobody asked me to run that check. That's just what QA means to me — not a gatekeeper, but the last line of care.

When I Broke the Build (On Purpose)

Manual testing shows you where things break. Automation shows you when — and that changes everything.Nobody tells you automation is humbling at first. You write a script, it fails, you fix it, it fails differently. But somewhere in that loop I stopped fighting the process and started trusting it — and that's when I actually became a better QA engineer. Breaking things on purpose, systematically, repeatedly — that's not chaos. That's how you build something you can actually stand behind.

The Test Case Nobody Wrote

Once I joined a project mid-sprint. No documentation. No test plan. Just a Figma file and a prayer. I mapped out 60+ test cases from scratch in two days, collaborating with the dev and design teams to fill the gaps. The product launched clean. That experience taught me that great QA isn't about following a checklist — it's about asking the right questions before the checklist even exists.
